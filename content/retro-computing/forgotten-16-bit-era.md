The way the 16-bit home-computer era is usually remembered - through the [Amiga](/retro-computing/what-the-amiga-got-right-that-were-still-copying/) on one hand and the early Mac on the other - leaves out three machines that mattered a great deal at the time and have been compressed almost entirely out of the popular memory. The Atari ST was a serious computer with a serious user base. The Acorn Archimedes shipped the first ARM processor and pioneered design ideas now standard. The Sharp X68000 was the most overbuilt home computer of its era, and the source of some of the best Japanese arcade ports ever made.

This is not nostalgia - all three machines had genuine, distinctive engineering merit, and parts of what they did got absorbed back into the mainstream long after their commercial lives ended.

## Atari ST: the computer that quietly ran music production

The Atari ST shipped in 1985 with a Motorola 68000 CPU, 512K of RAM, and a bitmap colour graphics system, at a price meaningfully below the original Macintosh and somewhat below the Amiga. The hardware was less ambitious than the Amiga's custom chip set, but the ST got two things very right.

First, **the built-in MIDI ports**. Every Atari ST had two DIN MIDI ports as standard equipment, at a time when adding MIDI to any other computer required an expensive interface card. This made the ST the de facto music production computer of the late 1980s. Cubase and Logic both originated on the ST. Tangerine Dream, Mike Oldfield, Vangelis, Fatboy Slim - the list of major artists who built careers on Atari ST sequencers is long, and the ST stayed in professional studios well into the 1990s, long after the platform had commercially declined.

Second, **TOS, the operating system**, was unusually well-engineered for its price tier. GEM, the desktop, was less elegant than the Mac's interface and more cluttered than the Amiga's Workbench, but it was solid, well-documented, and fast enough on the modest hardware. The ST was the cheapest computer in its era to run a real GUI productively.

The ST faded commercially in the early 1990s, but the music-production user base kept it relevant longer than the platform deserved on technical merit alone.

## Acorn Archimedes: the machine that shipped the future

The Acorn Archimedes is the most consequential computer most people have never heard of. Launched in 1987 by the British company [Acorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acorn_Computers), it shipped with the first ARM processor - a chip designed in-house at Acorn to replace the 6502 they had used in the BBC Micro. The Archimedes outperformed contemporary IBM PCs and Macs by significant margins in real benchmarks, often at lower prices.

The reason it matters is the chip. The ARM architecture Acorn developed for the Archimedes became Advanced RISC Machines Ltd in 1990, and ARM Holdings became the most important semiconductor company most consumers have never heard of. The chips in every smartphone, in every Apple Silicon Mac, in every Raspberry Pi, in most modern embedded devices - they all trace their lineage back to the Archimedes project. Acorn the computer company faded; ARM the chip designer became one of the largest forces in modern computing.

The Archimedes itself had a small but devoted user base, mostly in UK schools (BBC Micro inheritance) and among British technical users. RISC OS - the Archimedes operating system - is still maintained as an open-source project in 2026, mostly on Raspberry Pi hardware, where it remains the most direct functional descendant of the original.

## Sharp X68000: the home computer with arcade ambitions

The Sharp X68000 launched in Japan in 1987 and is the most over-engineered home computer of its era. The hardware specifications read like a serious workstation: Motorola 68000 at 10 MHz (later 16 MHz on the X68030), custom graphics hardware capable of multiple simultaneous layers and 65,536 colours from a 16.7-million palette, hardware sprites, FM and PCM sound, and SCSI built in.

What this hardware was actually used for, primarily, was arcade ports. The X68000 was the home machine of choice for Capcom, Konami, and Taito to release home conversions of their arcade titles. Daimakaimura (Ghouls 'n Ghosts), Street Fighter II, Final Fight, R-Type - the X68000 versions are often the most faithful home ports of their respective arcade originals, sometimes faithful enough that the X68000 itself was used as the development target.

The X68000 never sold outside Japan and was always expensive even within it. It is remembered today primarily by Japanese retro-computing enthusiasts and by the demoscene, which produced some of its most technically impressive work on the platform. A successor machine, the X68000 Z, was released in 2024 as a recreation hardware platform, suggesting the machine still has enough following to support a commercial revival project.

## What the survivors borrowed

The interesting thing about these three machines is how much of what they did was eventually absorbed by mainstream computing - just without credit to the originals.

The ARM architecture from the Archimedes is now the dominant processor design in consumer electronics. The MIDI-everywhere approach the ST pioneered became standard once USB MIDI was available. The graphics capabilities of the X68000, particularly the multi-layer sprite engine, prefigured what 32-bit consoles would offer half a decade later.

None of these machines won their commercial battles. All three contributed ideas that won their technological battles long after the machines themselves had been retired. That is a more interesting kind of victory than the one Apple and Commodore won in the same period, and one worth remembering.
