The conventional wisdom for the last decade was that hardware modular synthesis would eventually be eaten by software. Software modular environments - VCV Rack, Reaktor Blocks, Bitwig's Grid - kept getting better, cheaper, and more flexible. The hardware was beautiful, expensive, and constrained.

That story stopped being true sometime in 2024. Hardware modular is healthier in 2026 than at any point since the original modular era ended in the 1980s. The reasons are interesting and not obvious.

## What is actually happening

The Eurorack ecosystem has not just survived the software-modular wave - it has thrived alongside it. New module releases are at record pace. The boutique manufacturers are profitable. Used-market prices on classic modules have held or risen. The community is bigger, younger, and more diverse than it was five years ago.

A few things converged to produce this:

**Software modular taught a generation how to patch.** People who learned modular concepts on VCV Rack or Bitwig's Grid have moved to hardware as their primary instrument. The software was an accessibility ramp, not a replacement.

**The post-pandemic appetite for physical instruments.** After years of screen-based work, the tactile, hands-on nature of hardware modular has appeal that no software UI can match. The instrument-as-object matters in a way that screen-and-mouse does not.

**AI in music production has made hardware feel valuable in a different way.** When you can ask an AI to generate a track, the value of a unique, physical, hand-patched sound increases rather than decreases. Hardware modular produces sound that is unambiguously the player's, in a way that software increasingly does not.

## The manufacturers leading the resurgence

**Mutable Instruments** continued to define what high-quality, well-documented modular looks like, even after Emilie Gillet wound down the company - the open-source designs live on through clones and successor brands. The Plaits, Rings, and Marbles modules remain reference points.

**Make Noise** has stayed at the front of the experimental and West Coast modular world. The Maths module is still the single most-bought module in Eurorack and is in its third decade of production.

**Intellijel** owns the powered-case ecosystem and the well-engineered, utility-heavy end of the market. Their modules tend to be the ones that quietly become permanent in racks.

**ALM Busy Circuits** has emerged as one of the most interesting design houses in 2026, with modules like Pamela's Pro Workout and Mother becoming defaults in modern rigs.

**Erica Synths** and **Doepfer** continue to anchor the affordable and the original-formula ends of the market respectively. Doepfer in particular - the company that started Eurorack in 1995 - is still shipping new modules at thirty years in.

## What modular still does that software does not

The cases where hardware modular has a real edge in 2026:

**Sound design that benefits from real-world imperfection.** Analogue oscillators, real voltage variations, component drift, and the subtle non-linearities of physical circuits produce sounds that software can simulate but not quite duplicate.

**Patching as composition.** The constraint of having a finite number of cables and a finite number of inputs forces compositional choices that infinite software flexibility does not. Many producers report doing better creative work on hardware modular precisely because the constraints are real.

**Performance.** Live modular performance has texture and unpredictability that a laptop set does not. The instrument is visibly the instrument.

**Hands-on workflow.** The friction of physical patching - which is what software-modular tried hardest to remove - turns out to be valuable rather than bad. The slowness produces better decisions.

## Where the software-modular world still wins

This is not zero-sum, and the software side has not lost on every front:

- **Recallability.** Hardware patches do not save. Once you unpatch, that exact patch is gone unless you wrote it down.
- **Cost.** A serious modular rig costs thousands of pounds. VCV Rack is free.
- **Polyphony.** Polyphonic hardware modular exists but is expensive and rare. Software handles polyphony trivially.
- **Iteration speed.** Trying twenty oscillator algorithms in software takes a minute. Trying them in hardware takes a credit card.

The mature picture in 2026 is that software and hardware modular have stopped being competitors. They are complementary tools used by overlapping communities, often by the same producer. The producer designs patches in software, builds versions of those patches in hardware, performs the hardware version, and records the result.

## The unexpected consequence

The interesting consequence of the modular renaissance is that it has produced one of the more sustainable corners of the music industry. The companies are small, profitable, employee-owned in many cases, and focused on long product lifespans. Modules from 1996 are still in use; modules sold today will probably still be in use in 2050.

In a world where most music technology is software you rent and hardware that gets discontinued in three years, modular synthesis is producing instruments designed to last. That is unusual enough in 2026 to be its own reason for the resurgence.
