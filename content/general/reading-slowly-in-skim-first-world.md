Most reading in 2026 is not really reading. It is skimming - the eye sweeping for keywords, the brain pattern-matching for whatever the reader is trying to extract. The rest is summarisation - either by the reader after the fact or by an AI before the fact. Both modes are useful. Both are different from the older thing that "reading" used to mean.

There is a quieter practice underneath all of this that has not gone away, even though it gets less attention than it used to: reading a sentence at a time, at the pace the sentence was written, allowing the writer's thinking to assemble itself in the reader's head over hours rather than minutes. It is slower. It is less productive in any narrow sense. It is also one of the few cognitive practices that does what it does, and nothing else does it.

## What slow reading produces

A few things become available only when reading happens at the pace of the text rather than at the pace of the reader's extraction goals:

**The writer's actual argument, rather than a compressed version of it.** A good non-fiction writer is making moves at the level of paragraphs - setting up an objection here, conceding a point there, building toward a conclusion several pages away. Skimming loses the structure entirely. Summarising loses the weight of the argument's different parts. Reading slowly preserves both.

**The texture of how the writer thinks.** This matters more than it sounds like. Reading a great philosopher, scientist, or essayist at the pace they wrote produces familiarity with how their mind works. Over time the reader can predict the move the writer is about to make, which is the beginning of understanding their thought rather than just their conclusions.

**The reader's own time to react.** Slow reading produces moments where the reader stops, looks up, disagrees, agrees, thinks of an example, remembers something else. None of those happen at skim speed. The reader's own thinking is being trained alongside the reading.

**A different memory.** Slow-read material lands in long-term memory differently from skimmed material. Years later the reader can recall the structure of an argument they read once but read slowly, where they cannot recall any of the dozen blog posts they read quickly the same week.

## Why it has become harder

The infrastructure has rotated against slow reading in 2026 in ways worth being explicit about.

The reading material itself is increasingly written for skimming - subheadings every two paragraphs, bullet points where prose would have been, "TL;DR" sections at the top. This is responsive to how people actually read online, and it produces good skimmable content. It is also harder to read slowly - the structure has been pre-extracted in a way that defeats the slow-reading practice of letting the structure emerge.

The reading devices push toward speed. Phones, tablets, and Kindles all surface "reading time remaining" and "pages per minute" and progress percentages that reframe reading as something to be completed rather than experienced. The well-designed reading environment has become a productivity tool.

AI summarisation is now ambient. The link to the long essay arrives with a one-paragraph summary attached, or the reader's email client offers to summarise the article before they open it. Most of the time they take the offer. The unsummarised reading experience has become something the reader has to actively choose against the default.

## What it takes to do it

Slow reading does not require much equipment, but it does require some choices that are no longer the default:

**A physical book or a stripped-down digital reader.** The web browser is a poor slow-reading environment. The Kindle with progress and speed indicators turned off is fine. The Remarkable, Kobo, and similar e-ink devices have become quietly popular among slow readers in 2026 because they push less against the practice than the multipurpose tablet does.

**A defined time, not a defined pace.** "I will read for 45 minutes" works. "I will read this much" does not, because the temptation to speed up to make the page count overwhelms the slow-reading practice.

**Material worth reading slowly.** A lot of the writing produced in 2026 does not reward slow reading - it was written to be skimmed and skimming is the appropriate response. The materials that reward slow reading are mostly older, mostly book-length, and mostly by writers who took their time.

**Acceptance that this is slower than the alternatives.** A 300-page book read at slow-reading pace is a project of weeks. The reader who insists on finishing things quickly is in the wrong practice.

## What it is not

This is not an argument that slow reading is morally superior or that fast reading is wrong. Skimming is the correct response to most internet content. AI summarisation is genuinely useful for navigating large bodies of work to find the few pieces worth reading slowly. The faster modes of reading exist for good reasons.

The argument is just that slow reading is a different thing from skimming or summarising, and it produces something - the texture of another mind, the experience of a long argument, the formation of memory that lasts - that the other modes do not. Losing it without replacing it is a real loss, not a productivity gain.

In a year when more text is being produced, distributed, and consumed than at any previous point in history, the cognitive practice of reading slowly has become more unusual and more valuable, not less. It is the kind of attention that does not get easier with practice but does get rarer. Worth keeping.
