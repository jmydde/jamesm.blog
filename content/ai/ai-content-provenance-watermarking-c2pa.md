The question "is this AI-generated?" stopped being abstract some time in 2024 and became operational in 2025. Newsrooms, courts, election regulators, social media platforms, and individual users all started asking it about specific pieces of content. The answers, in 2026, are still messier than the marketing around provenance tools suggests - but the infrastructure for answering them has moved meaningfully forward, and the legal and policy landscape has stopped pretending the problem will solve itself.

## The pieces of the puzzle

There are three distinct technical approaches in serious deployment, each addressing a different part of the question:

**Content credentials** ([C2PA](https://c2pa.org/)) attach cryptographic signatures to content at creation time. A camera, an AI generator, or an editing tool signs the file with metadata about who created it and what changes have been made. This is forensic-grade attribution when the chain is intact.

**Model-level watermarks** embed statistical signatures into AI-generated content that are invisible to humans but detectable by inspection tools. Major image generators, video generators, and increasingly text generators ship with watermarks of varying strengths.

**Post-hoc detection** uses machine learning to classify content as AI-generated based on artefacts the generator left behind. This is the noisiest approach - sometimes useful, often unreliable, increasingly unreliable as generators improve.

These approaches are complementary, not substitutes. Each works in different scenarios and fails in different ways.

## What C2PA actually does in 2026

C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) has matured into something close to its original ambition. The standard is now supported by most major camera manufacturers, major AI image and video generators, and the editing tools in the Adobe and DaVinci ecosystems. Content created in a supporting tool gets a cryptographic certificate; modifications get appended to that certificate; the final content can be inspected to see its provenance chain.

When the chain is intact, this is genuinely useful. A news organisation receiving a tip-off photo can verify that it was taken on a real camera, see what edits have been made, and confirm the source. A court can verify that a piece of evidence was generated by a specific tool at a specific time. A platform can choose to label content based on its provenance.

The limitations are also real. The chain is fragile - any platform that strips metadata (most social platforms do) breaks it. The signatures verify the creator, not the truth of the content. A camera can sign a staged scene as authentically photographed. The system is for authentication of provenance, not for authentication of reality.

## Where model watermarking has gone

The model-watermarking side has had a harder year. The 2024 marketing about robust watermarks that would let platforms reliably detect AI content has met the empirical reality that watermarks are removable, transferable, and fragile.

For images, watermarks survive basic operations but can usually be removed by determined adversaries. For text, watermarks are unreliable across paraphrasing. For audio and video, the watermarks are improving but the same arms race exists. The 2026 state of the art is that watermarking provides probabilistic evidence of AI origin, useful for honest content creators but not a defence against motivated bad actors.

The major model providers have continued shipping watermarks anyway, on the grounds that they catch the easy cases and demonstrate good-faith effort even when they cannot catch the hard cases. Anthropic, OpenAI, Google, and Meta all watermark by default in 2026. The standards for what counts as a sufficient watermark are still being negotiated.

## What the policy environment looks like

The regulatory side has caught up faster than the technical side has. The EU AI Act provisions on AI content labelling came into force in 2025. The US has produced an executive-order-level framework that most major platforms are voluntarily complying with. The UK has its own variation. China has its own much stricter version.

The practical effect: most major platforms now label content they identify as AI-generated, most major generators apply watermarks by default, and most major regulated industries (finance, healthcare, government) require provenance documentation for any AI-generated material used in formal capacity.

This has not solved the deepfake problem. It has made the responsible end of the AI content ecosystem traceable, which is meaningful even if it does not cover the actors who choose not to participate.

## Where the gaps are

The honest assessment of where this leaves us in 2026 includes some uncomfortable acknowledgements:

- **Sophisticated adversaries can defeat all current technical measures.** The systems work against the casual misuse case, not against the motivated state-actor case.
- **Most content travels through platforms that strip metadata.** C2PA chains break on most uploads. Until the platforms preserve provenance metadata - which they have economic reasons not to do - the chain is mostly broken in practice.
- **The detection arms race favours generation.** Generation improves continuously; detection improves intermittently. The long-term trend is toward harder-to-detect AI content, not easier.
- **The legal frameworks treat AI content as a separate category.** This creates a strong incentive to make AI content look like human content, which works against transparency.

## The pattern that is emerging

What seems to be emerging in 2026 is a layered approach where no single technical measure is expected to solve the problem alone, but the combination provides enough friction and enough partial coverage to handle the common cases:

- **Trust the chain when it exists.** Content with valid C2PA credentials can be treated as authenticated, with the caveats about what authentication means.
- **Treat unwatermarked content as ambiguous.** Default scepticism toward unverified content from unknown sources.
- **Use post-hoc detection as a screening tool.** Useful for filtering at scale, unreliable for individual cases.
- **Rely on context and source.** The most durable answer to "is this real" remains the same as it was before AI: where did it come from, who is showing it to me, do they have a reason to deceive.

The technology has improved meaningfully in two years. It is still not a substitute for the older practices of journalistic verification, legal evidence chains, and social trust. What it has done is raise the baseline - some of the easy cases that were tools for confusion in 2023 are now solvable. The hard cases remain hard, and the right response to that is probably to acknowledge it rather than to oversell what the tools can do.
