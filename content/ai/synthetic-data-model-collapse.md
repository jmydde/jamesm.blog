The marketing story about frontier AI in 2026 is about capability gains. The quieter story underneath is about where the training data is going to come from. The labs have publicly known since 2023 that high-quality human text is finite and that the frontier was on track to consume effectively all of it. The empirical fact, in 2026, is that the consumption has happened, that the response has been a sharp move toward synthetic data, and that synthetic data has its own problems serious enough to have been named in the research literature. The label is model collapse. The risk is real. The labs' answers are getting better but they are not complete.

## TL;DR

- The supply of high-quality human-authored text on the public internet is finite and the frontier labs have largely consumed it. Several credible projections put the [exhaustion point around 2026](https://invisibletech.ai/blog/ai-training-in-2026-anchoring-synthetic-data-in-human-truth).
- The response has been a sharp move toward **synthetic data** - text generated by existing AI models, used to train new models. By 2026 a significant fraction of the training data used by frontier labs is synthetic by some measure.
- The risk is **[model collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_collapse)**: a degenerative process where models trained on synthetic data progressively lose the long tail of the distribution, overestimate common cases, and underestimate rare-but-important ones. The phenomenon was documented in a [2024 *Nature* paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07566-y) and has been reproduced in multiple subsequent studies.
- The mechanism is not subtle. Each generation of synthetic data is sampled from the model that produced it, which means probable outputs are over-represented and the rare tails of the original human distribution are progressively lost. The collapse is **slow, cumulative, and hard to detect from inside the training loop**.
- The labs have developed real mitigations: **provenance tracking** (knowing which data is human vs synthetic), **synthetic-ratio capping** (limiting how much of the corpus is generated), **verification-based generation** (only keeping synthetic outputs that pass external checks), and **human-anchored evaluation**.
- The mitigations work but they are not free. The cost of running a clean training pipeline is higher than the cost of running a contaminated one, and the labs that take it seriously are spending real money on the problem. The labs that do not are exposing themselves to capability regressions that may not become visible for several model generations.

## Why human data ran out

The data-exhaustion story is older than the model-collapse story. It is worth getting straight because the two are connected and they are sometimes conflated.

The amount of high-quality text on the public internet is bounded. It is large but it is not unlimited. Various estimates from the early 2020s put the upper bound on usable English-language web text at roughly 10 to 30 trillion tokens, depending on how aggressively you define "usable." The frontier training runs of 2023-2024 were already using a meaningful fraction of this. The runs of 2025-2026 are using nearly all of it. The growth rate of human text generation on the open web (perhaps a few percent per year) is much slower than the growth rate of training-set demand (an order of magnitude per couple of years), and the gap has been visible in the trajectory of the labs' data curation strategies for at least three years.

The credible projections of when high-quality human text would run out clustered around 2026 when they were first made in 2022-2023. The empirical evidence in 2026 is that they were roughly right. The frontier labs have moved aggressively to acquire non-public text (licensing deals with publishers, scientific journal archives, books corpora, code repositories under specific licences) and to expand into other modalities (video, audio, structured data). But the core text corpus has been largely consumed, and the marginal token of net-new high-quality human text is now expensive to acquire and slow to produce.

The result is that the labs have had to find alternative sources of training data. The dominant alternative is synthetic data - text generated by existing AI models. The synthetic-data response has worked well enough to be the default in 2025-2026 but it is not without serious risks.

## What model collapse actually is

Model collapse is the name for the degenerative process that happens when models are trained on data produced by other models, particularly when this happens recursively across generations.

The [foundational paper](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-024-07566-y), published in *Nature* in 2024 by Ilia Shumailov and colleagues, established the phenomenon experimentally. They trained models on a mix of human-authored and synthetic data, then used those models to generate the next round of training data, and so on across multiple generations. The empirical result was that the models progressively lost the long tail of the original distribution. Rare-but-important cases got under-sampled in each generation. Probable cases got over-sampled. The errors compounded across generations until the model's outputs no longer resembled the original distribution at all.

The mechanism is not particularly mysterious. When a model generates text, it samples from its own learned distribution. The samples it produces are concentrated in the high-probability regions of that distribution. When you train a new model on those samples, the new model's distribution is shifted toward those high-probability regions. The next round of sampling concentrates further. The long tail - the rare, the unusual, the contextually specific - is progressively lost.

The reason the phenomenon matters in practice is that the long tail is where most of the interesting capability lives. The frontier models are not impressive because they do common tasks well - they are impressive because they handle the unusual cases, the novel framings, the requests that do not look like anything in the training data. If the training pipeline systematically loses the tail, the resulting models lose the ability to handle exactly the cases that matter most.

The other reason it matters is that the collapse is slow and hard to detect from inside the training loop. Standard validation metrics often improve as the distribution narrows - the model becomes more confident, the loss on common cases drops, the generation looks cleaner. The degradation only becomes visible on benchmarks specifically designed to test tail behaviour, and those benchmarks are exactly the ones that the [eval problems](/ai/ai-evals-are-broken/) discussed elsewhere have made harder to trust.

## The current state of synthetic-data practice

The frontier labs have not stopped using synthetic data. They have got more careful about how they use it. The current state of practice has several components.

**Provenance tracking.** The serious labs now tag every document in their training corpus with its source category: human-authored, human-edited (a model wrote a draft and a human revised it), synthetic (model-generated, possibly model-filtered), unknown. The tagging is imperfect but it allows the labs to control the ratios at training time, which is the first prerequisite for any meaningful mitigation.

**Synthetic-ratio capping.** Most frontier labs now cap the fraction of synthetic data in the training corpus at some maximum level - the published numbers are hard to come by but the credible estimates cluster in the 20-40% range for the most careful labs. The cap is a coarse instrument but it prevents the worst form of recursive degradation.

**Verification-based generation.** The most sophisticated synthetic-data pipelines do not just generate text and add it to the corpus. They generate text and check it against external sources - search results, structured knowledge bases, code execution, mathematical verification - and only keep the outputs that pass. The approach is expensive but it largely eliminates the recursive-degradation risk because the synthetic data is anchored to external truth rather than to the model's own distribution.

**Human-anchored evaluation.** The labs that take the collapse problem seriously maintain large evaluation suites of human-authored text and human-judged outputs as the gold standard. Models are not promoted to production until they perform well on these human-anchored evals, regardless of how they perform on automatic metrics that might themselves be contaminated by the synthetic distribution.

**Diverse data sourcing.** The frontier labs have invested heavily in expanding the non-public-internet sources of training data - books, journal archives, code, licensed media, structured data, video transcripts, audio transcripts. The diversity is itself a mitigation against collapse because data from different sources has different distributional properties and resists the narrowing dynamic.

The interesting thing is that these mitigations work. The capability trajectory of the frontier models has continued to improve through 2025-2026, despite the data exhaustion and the synthetic-data risk. The labs that take it seriously are not seeing the catastrophic collapse the early papers warned about. The collapse story is real but it is being managed by the players who are spending the money on managing it.

## Where the mitigations are not enough

The mitigations have limits and the limits are worth understanding.

**Cost.** The verification-based generation pipelines are expensive. Generating synthetic data with verification can cost an order of magnitude more per useful token than naive generation, and the labs that operate with smaller budgets are forced into corners that increase their collapse risk. The smaller open-weight labs in particular face a real cost constraint here, and the empirical risk of collapse in their training pipelines is higher than it is in the frontier proprietary labs.

**Tail detection.** The standard validation metrics do not reliably detect tail degradation. Building evaluations that catch it requires deliberate investment in tail-specific benchmarks, in long-tail human evaluation, and in production monitoring that watches for capability regressions on rare cases. The labs that do this catch the problems early. The labs that do not, do not, and the failure mode is that the capability regression appears in production as a slow drift rather than as a sudden break.

**Generation contamination.** A subtle problem is that even careful synthetic-data pipelines can be contaminated by other people's synthetic data. The public internet in 2026 contains a significant and growing fraction of AI-generated text - blog posts, social media content, news articles, product reviews. If a lab's "human" web data is in fact mostly AI-generated, the lab is doing recursive training without knowing it. The detection of AI-generated text is itself an open research problem and the false-negative rate on the best current detectors is high enough to be a real concern.

**Reasoning-trace synthetic data.** A specific case worth highlighting is the use of synthetic reasoning traces in [reasoning model training](/ai/reasoning-models-2026/). The current training recipe for reasoning models often involves generating large quantities of reasoning traces with one model and using them to train the next. This is the form of synthetic data most at risk of recursive degradation, and the empirical evidence on whether the current generation of reasoning models has been affected is mixed. The labs claim it has not. The independent verification of that claim is harder than it should be.

## The controversial parts

Three claims in the synthetic-data conversation deserve more pushback than they typically get.

The first is the claim that model collapse is no longer a real risk because the labs have figured it out. The empirical evidence in 2026 is that the labs with significant resources have figured out enough of the problem to keep shipping good models. That is not the same as having solved it. The collapse risk is being managed, not eliminated, and the long-run trajectory depends on whether the mitigations continue to keep pace with the increasing reliance on synthetic data.

The second is the claim that synthetic data is just as good as human data if you generate it carefully. The claim is partially true - high-quality synthetic data can be valuable for many tasks. The claim is not fully true because the distributional richness of human-authored text reflects the distributional richness of human experience, and the synthetic substitute does not have access to that source. The labs that treat synthetic data as fully equivalent to human data are taking a bet that the empirical record does not yet support.

The third is the claim that the data-exhaustion problem will be solved by multimodal training. Video, audio, and image data are real and growing, and the labs have invested heavily in them. The multimodal data does help. It does not, however, fully substitute for the kind of textual reasoning capability that comes from training on dense, structured, argument-laden human text - the kind of text that has been the foundation of the language-model capability curve. The multimodal expansion buys time and adds capability but it does not eliminate the underlying text-exhaustion problem.

## Where this is heading

The most likely shape of 2027-2028 is that the synthetic-data question becomes a more public part of the AI conversation than it has been so far. The labs will continue to invest in mitigations. Some labs will be more careful than others, and the capability differences between them will increasingly reflect data-pipeline quality rather than just compute budget or model architecture. The auditing of training-data provenance will become a more important question for procurement and for regulation, particularly in jurisdictions that take AI quality requirements seriously.

The other prediction worth making is that the open-weight ecosystem is going to feel the collapse problem more acutely than the frontier proprietary labs. The mitigations are expensive, the open labs have smaller budgets, and the public web - the dominant training source for many open projects - is increasingly contaminated with AI-generated text. The capability gap between open-weight and proprietary-frontier may widen on dimensions related to long-tail handling, even as it narrows on the headline benchmarks where the contamination is less consequential.

For people building with AI today, the practical implications are less direct than for many of the other topics in this series. The model selection decision is downstream of the labs' data pipelines, and you mostly cannot see those. The signals that matter are the tail-behaviour evaluations - how does the model do on unusual cases, on novel framings, on requests that do not look like training data - and the rate at which the labs are willing to disclose what is in their training corpora. Both of these will become more important questions over the next two years.

The deeper point is that the AI capability curve depends on something the marketing narrative rarely mentions: the quality and diversity of the training data. The capability gains of the last decade were powered by an extraordinarily rich source - the entire accumulated written record of humanity, mostly free, mostly unconstrained, mostly trusted. That source has been consumed. What comes next depends on how well the labs can build sustainable alternatives, and the empirical record so far is partial. The collapse risk is real, the mitigations are real, the cost is real, and the trajectory is real. The story is quieter than the headline capability story but it may matter more.
