The International Space Station's planned end-of-life around 2030 has put a hard deadline on a question that has been circling NASA for years: what does continuous American presence in low Earth orbit look like after the ISS? The bet NASA placed - that commercial operators would build replacements rather than the agency funding a successor outright - is now midway through being tested. Four serious projects are in development, with real money, real hardware, and real schedules. Whether any of them will be flying habitable stations by the ISS's actual retirement is the open question.

## The four serious contenders

**Vast** has moved from outsider to front-runner in 2026 in part by being the most realistic about scope. The Haven-1 station - a single-module commercial outpost intended to launch in 2026 on a SpaceX Falcon 9 - was the first commercial station to reach hardware-ready status. Larger Haven-2 follows. Vast's approach has been to ship small, fly real hardware, and learn from operations rather than designing the perfect station on paper for a decade.

**Axiom Space** has been visiting the ISS with crewed missions since 2022 and is building modules intended to attach to the ISS before separating to form a free-flying station. The "attach first, separate later" strategy is conservative on engineering risk and aggressive on schedule risk - the ISS retirement timeline has compressed Axiom's window.

**Starlab** (Voyager Space and Airbus) is the more traditional approach - a single-launch, fully-equipped commercial station intended to fly on a SpaceX Starship. The dependency on Starship being routinely operational creates schedule risk that Vast does not have, but the resulting station is more capable.

**Orbital Reef** (Blue Origin, Sierra Space, and partners) is the most ambitious of the four - a "mixed-use business park" in orbit, with multiple modules supporting both crewed habitation and commercial research bays. It is also the furthest behind. The 2026 status is "still substantially on paper."

## NASA's transition plan

NASA's Commercial LEO Development programme funds three of these (Vast is funded separately) at a level that is meaningful but not enough to ensure success on its own. The agency is explicitly trying to act as an anchor customer rather than a primary funder - the bet is that commercial demand for orbital research and tourism will fund the bulk of the station, with NASA buying crew time as one customer among several.

The question that nobody has cleanly answered is whether that commercial demand actually exists at the volume needed. The ISS has hosted commercial research, manufacturing experiments, and the occasional tourist - but the volume has been modest. If NASA is the only serious customer for orbital habitation after ISS, the commercial-station model under-performs the cost projections it was built on.

## What is actually flying

The 2026 hardware status:

- **Haven-1** (Vast): Module integration largely complete; on track for launch in late 2026 or early 2027.
- **Axiom modules**: First module is in advanced manufacturing; launch dates have slipped from original 2024 targets to 2027-2028 timeframe.
- **Starlab**: Still in design refinement; flight not realistic before 2028-2029 at earliest.
- **Orbital Reef**: Critical Design Review in progress; flight beyond 2029.

The realistic picture: only Vast has a station likely to be operational while the ISS is still flying. The others are racing the ISS retirement.

## What could go wrong

The risks to the commercial-station model are not principally technical - they are economic and schedule:

**Demand uncertainty.** If pharmaceutical research, materials science, and commercial tourism do not produce sustained demand at the projected levels, the operators face a difficult choice between operating at a loss or scaling down.

**Launch capacity.** A meaningful share of station operations depend on Starship or similar large lift becoming routinely operational. Schedule slips on the launch side ripple through the station side.

**NASA budget shifts.** Commercial-station economics depend on NASA being a stable anchor customer. Any major budget reshuffle - towards Artemis, towards Mars, away from LEO - threatens the model.

**ISS extension.** If the ISS retirement slips to 2032 or 2033, the commercial stations have time but the urgency that has driven funding decisions also weakens.

## The bigger picture

The shift from a government-built space station to commercial-built ones is the most significant institutional change in American human spaceflight since the end of the Shuttle programme. If it works, it produces a more sustainable model where multiple competing operators serve a broader market. If it does not, the US ends up without continuous LEO presence for the first time in three decades.

The international politics matter too. China's [Tiangong station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiangong_space_station) is operational and being expanded. India and Russia are both working on national-scale stations. The US betting on commercial-only LEO is a significant policy bet that has not been seriously stress-tested.

The 2026 view is cautiously optimistic on at least one or two of the four projects flying real hardware by 2030. The honest view is that nobody knows yet which two.
