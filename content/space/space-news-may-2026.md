## TL;DR

- **Starship is flying again.** After a seven-month gap, SpaceX is set to launch the first flight of its redesigned **Starship Version 3** on 21 May - the first Starship launch of 2026 and a major test for the vehicle NASA wants as its Moon lander. As this post goes out, the launch has not yet happened, so the result is still unknown.
- **SMILE is in orbit.** A joint **ESA and Chinese Academy of Sciences** mission launched on 19 May to give scientists their first global, X-ray view of how the solar wind batters Earth's magnetic shield.
- **A Cargo Dragon flew for the sixth time.** NASA's **34th SpaceX resupply run** to the International Space Station on 15 May was the first time a cargo capsule has flown six missions - a quiet but real reusability milestone.
- **China kept its station stocked.** **Tianzhou-10** delivered roughly 6.2 tonnes of supplies to the Tiangong station on 11 May and will stay docked for a record 12 months.
- **Webb drew the clearest map yet of the cosmic web.** Using 164,000 galaxies, the **COSMOS-Web** survey traced the vast filaments of gas and dark matter that connect galaxies back to when the universe was about a billion years old.

It has been a busy month, and the shape of it is worth noticing. There is no single dramatic breakthrough here. Instead there is a steady drumbeat of launches, resupply runs and survey science - the kind of month that shows spaceflight quietly turning into infrastructure. Here is what actually mattered, with the marketing filtered out.

## 1. Starship V3 returns to the pad

The headline event of the month is a test flight that, at the time of writing, has not yet flown. SpaceX is set to launch the [first flight of Starship Version 3](https://www.space.com/news/live/spacex-starship-flight-12-launch-updates-may-21-2026) on 21 May, with the launch window opening in the evening, Florida time. It is the first Starship launch of 2026 and ends a roughly seven-month gap since Flight 11 last October.

This is not a small iteration. Version 3 is a substantial redesign of both the Super Heavy booster and the upper stage, and a clean first flight is the whole point of the exercise. The plan for this mission is suborbital: the upper stage follows an arc across the Indian Ocean, deploys 20 dummy Starlink satellites - two of them carrying cameras to photograph the ship's heat shield in flight - and then both the booster and the upper stage perform controlled splashdowns rather than the dramatic tower catches SpaceX has shown before. That choice is sensible. On a maiden flight of a new build, you want data, not theatrics.

The stakes are higher than a normal test. NASA is relying on a crewed variant of Starship as the lander that will carry astronauts to the lunar surface under the [Artemis programme](https://www.nasa.gov/humans-in-space/artemis/). Every month the vehicle spends grounded is a month of schedule pressure on the Moon landings. SpaceX had a bruising run of test failures through 2025, so the V3 redesign is best read as a reset rather than a victory lap.

The honest take: treat this as a genuine unknown. A first flight of a heavily redesigned rocket can end in a calm splashdown or in a fireball over the Gulf, and both outcomes still count as data. What matters for the wider programme is not whether this single flight is flawless, but whether V3 shows that the redesign fixed the problems that dogged the previous version. We will know more within days.

## 2. SMILE lifts off to watch Earth's magnetic shield

On 19 May, the [SMILE spacecraft launched](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Space_Science/Smile/Smile_lifts_off_on_quest_to_reveal_Earth_s_invisible_shield_against_the_solar_wind) on a Vega-C rocket from Europe's spaceport in French Guiana. SMILE - the Solar wind Magnetosphere Ionosphere Link Explorer - is a joint mission between the [European Space Agency](https://www.esa.int/Science_Exploration/Space_Science/Smile) and the Chinese Academy of Sciences, the product of roughly 25 years of cooperation between the two organisations.

What makes SMILE genuinely novel is its method. The magnetosphere - the magnetic bubble that deflects the solar wind and protects the planet - is invisible to ordinary instruments. SMILE will image it in soft X-rays, which are produced when charged particles from the Sun collide with neutral atoms at the edge of Earth's magnetic field. Combined with an ultraviolet camera that watches the auroras, the mission is designed to capture, for the first time, a continuous global picture of the whole Sun-Earth interaction rather than the keyhole views that single-point probes provide.

From a highly elliptical orbit reaching far above the North Pole, SMILE will spend about a month travelling to its operational orbit before commissioning its instruments, with routine science expected to begin later in the year. That makes it a slow-burn story - there will be no spectacular images for a while. But it is the kind of mission that pays off in better space-weather forecasting, which matters for satellites, power grids and aviation. It is also a reminder that international science collaborations can still get hardware to orbit even when the geopolitical mood is frosty.

## 3. A Cargo Dragon flew for the sixth time

On 15 May, a Falcon 9 lifted off from Cape Canaveral carrying SpaceX's Dragon on [NASA's 34th commercial resupply mission](https://www.nasa.gov/news-release/nasa-science-cargo-launch-on-34th-spacex-resupply-mission-to-station/) to the International Space Station, with roughly 6,500 pounds of supplies and experiments aboard. The science manifest was the usual eclectic mix: a wood-based scaffold being tested for bone-loss research, an instrument to measure the charged particles that disrupt power grids and satellites, and hardware for studying how spaceflight changes red blood cells.

The detail that makes this more than a routine logistics run is the hardware. According to [Spaceflight Now's launch coverage](https://spaceflightnow.com/2026/05/12/live-coverage-nasa-spacex-to-launch-34th-cargo-dragon-mission-to-the-space-station/), the Dragon capsule on this flight, serial number C209, was making its sixth trip to orbit - the first time a cargo Dragon has flown six missions. The Falcon 9 booster underneath it, B1096, was also on its sixth flight.

This is the sort of story that gets very little attention precisely because it works. A decade ago, flying a spacecraft six times would have been a programme-defining achievement. In 2026 it is a line item in a launch blog. That normalisation is the real headline: reuse has moved from a bold engineering bet to the unremarkable economics of how cargo gets to the station. It is also worth remembering that the ISS itself is in its final years, with retirement planned around 2030, so each of these flights is keeping useful science going on a platform with a firm expiry date.

## 4. China's Tianzhou-10 restocks Tiangong

On 11 May, a Long March 7 rocket lifted off from the Wenchang launch site carrying [Tianzhou-10](https://www.globalsecurity.org/space/library/news/2026/space-260511-globaltimes01.htm), an uncrewed cargo freighter bound for China's Tiangong space station. It reached the station and docked with the aft port of the Tianhe core module just a few hours later, in the kind of rapid rendezvous that has become routine for the programme.

The freighter delivered roughly 6.2 tonnes of cargo across more than 220 separate items: a new set of extravehicular spacesuits, a fresh treadmill, around 700 kilograms of propellant, and six scientific payloads - the most ever carried on a single Tianzhou flight. The supplies are intended to support the Shenzhou-23 and Shenzhou-24 crews. One notable change is the docked duration: Tianzhou-10 is planned to stay attached for up to 12 months, longer than any previous cargo ship in the programme.

The story here is consistency. This was the tenth consecutive successful Tianzhou mission, and China has settled into a dependable cadence of keeping Tiangong crewed and supplied. Set against an ageing ISS, the contrast is instructive: one station is winding down while another is hitting its stride. Whatever one thinks of the wider space rivalry, a steady resupply rhythm is a sign of a mature, operational programme rather than a series of one-off demonstrations.

## 5. Webb maps the cosmic web in unprecedented detail

The month's best pure-science story came from the James Webb Space Telescope. Astronomers released [the most detailed map yet of the cosmic web](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260511213136.htm) - the enormous lattice of gas and dark matter filaments that links galaxies across the universe, threaded between vast and nearly empty voids.

The map is the product of COSMOS-Web, the largest General Observer programme [Webb](https://science.nasa.gov/mission/webb/) has run, covering a patch of sky roughly the size of three full Moons. By analysing more than 164,000 galaxies and measuring their distances far more precisely than earlier surveys could, the team was able to place each galaxy into the correct slice of cosmic time. That extra precision is what sharpens the picture: it lets astronomers trace the web's structure back to when the universe was only about a billion years old.

Why it matters: the cosmic web is the scaffolding the entire universe is built on, and how galaxies grow depends heavily on where they sit within it. A sharper map is a sharper test of our models of structure formation and dark matter. The research team also released the underlying data, catalogue and visualisations publicly, which means the result will keep generating science well beyond the original paper. It is a good example of Webb doing the unglamorous, foundational work - not a single startling object, but a better atlas of everything.

## The honest take

Strip out the noise and this was a month of cadence rather than spectacle. Two resupply missions, a science mission slipping quietly into orbit, a survey result, and one big test flight whose outcome is still pending. None of it is the kind of news that stops traffic. All of it is the kind of news that shows a maturing industry.

If there is a single thread, it is that routine is the achievement. A cargo capsule on its sixth flight, a tenth straight Tianzhou success, an ESA-China science mission reaching the pad despite the politics - these are the markers of spaceflight becoming dependable infrastructure rather than a sequence of heroic firsts. The one genuine wildcard is Starship V3, and we will know how that went before this post is a week old. Check back: if the redesign holds up, the next month's round-up looks very different.
