For most of the crypto era, "tokenising real-world assets" was the persistent promise of every cycle. Every two years a new wave of projects would claim that real estate, treasuries, art, commodities, or anything else was about to move on-chain. Almost none of them produced sustained adoption. The combination of regulatory uncertainty, custodial weakness, and lack of institutional appetite kept the actual volume tiny relative to the rhetoric.

That has changed in 2026, but quietly and unevenly. Some specific RWA categories have crossed the threshold from "interesting experiment" to "operationally significant infrastructure." Others remain stuck where they have always been. The pattern of which is which says something interesting about what actually works for tokenisation and what does not.

## Where it is actually working

The categories where on-chain tokenisation has reached meaningful scale in 2026:

**US Treasury bills.** Tokenised treasuries are now a multi-billion-dollar category, with serious institutional issuers (BlackRock, Franklin Templeton, Ondo Finance, Hashnote). The product is fundamentally simple - a token that represents shares in a money market fund holding short-term US debt - but the wrapping in on-chain form provides 24/7 settlement, integration with DeFi infrastructure, and faster on/off ramps than the traditional bond settlement system.

**Private credit.** Lending against real-world business cash flows, secured by traditional collateral, structured on-chain. Maple Finance, Centrifuge, Goldfinch, and several others have built real loan books at scale. The borrowers are mostly emerging-market businesses and crypto-native operators; the lenders include both crypto-native pools and traditional institutional capital looking for yield.

**Trade finance.** Tokenised invoices, letters of credit, and supply-chain financing instruments. This has been an active area for years; it has reached meaningful volume in 2026 mostly because the underlying inefficiencies in the traditional system are large enough to justify the on-chain alternative.

**Money market funds.** Adjacent to treasuries but broader - tokenised exposure to short-term high-quality fixed income. Several large asset managers now offer these, and the on-chain wrapper provides faster movement than the traditional fund-share infrastructure.

## Where it has not worked

The categories that have continued to disappoint:

**Real estate tokenisation.** The pitch has been around for a decade. The reality remains that fractional ownership of real estate via tokens does not solve the fundamental problems with real estate as an asset class - illiquidity, jurisdictional complexity, management cost, regulatory friction. Tokens make secondary trading marginally easier; they do not solve any of the harder problems.

**Art tokenisation.** Distinct from NFTs of digital art - this is tokens representing fractional ownership of physical artworks. The market exists but has not scaled. The asset class is too idiosyncratic, the valuation too subjective, and the custody too dependent on trusted intermediaries for the on-chain wrapper to add much.

**Commodities.** Tokenised gold has reached meaningful scale (Paxos's PAXG, Tether's XAUT) but tokenised oil, agricultural commodities, and industrial metals have not. The combination of physical custody complexity and existing well-functioning commodity exchanges has kept on-chain volumes small.

**Equities.** Tokenised stocks (Backed, Dinari, several others) exist and trade. The volumes remain small because the regulatory frameworks around securities trading make the on-chain version less attractive than the traditional one for most use cases.

## The pattern of what works

Looking across the categories that have worked and the ones that have not, a pattern emerges:

**Tokenisation works for assets that are already abstract.** Treasuries are paper claims on cash flows; tokenising them is moving a paper abstraction into a digital abstraction. The underlying asset does not change.

**Tokenisation does not work for assets that are physical and idiosyncratic.** Real estate, art, commodities - the on-chain wrapper does not address what makes these hard. The trust questions, custody questions, and valuation questions remain after tokenisation.

**Tokenisation works when it competes with traditional financial infrastructure that is genuinely slow or expensive.** Treasury settlement, trade finance, private credit origination - these are areas where the traditional rails have real friction and the on-chain alternative provides measurable improvement.

**Tokenisation does not work when the traditional infrastructure is already efficient.** Public equity trading, FX, futures markets - these are areas where the existing systems are very efficient, and on-chain alternatives have to provide significant additional value to compete.

The cases where tokenisation has worked are the ones where the on-chain version is *operationally better* than the alternative, not just *more decentralised*.

## The regulatory picture

The regulatory environment has evolved meaningfully in 2026:

**The US** has produced workable frameworks for tokenised securities under existing securities law, with clearer guidance on broker-dealer registration, custody, and exchange listing for tokenised products. The remaining ambiguity is significant but less than it was three years ago.

**The EU** has MiCA in force, with provisions that cover tokenised assets clearly enough that institutional issuers can operate compliantly within the bloc.

**Singapore and Hong Kong** have continued to be the most innovation-friendly major financial centres, with regulatory frameworks specifically designed to attract tokenised asset business.

**The UK** has its own post-Brexit framework that is broadly compatible with the EU approach.

The combined picture is that compliant tokenised RWA issuance is now a regulated financial-services activity in the major economies, with established firms participating and clear paths to compliance for new entrants. This is dramatically different from the "we hope nobody enforces against us" posture that defined the earlier years.

## What is interesting going forward

A few things to watch in the next eighteen months:

**Cross-chain composability for tokenised assets.** A tokenised treasury issued on Ethereum should be usable as collateral on Solana or Avalanche. The infrastructure for this is improving but is not yet routine. When it becomes routine, the use cases expand significantly.

**Integration with traditional finance settlement.** When tokenised securities can settle directly against bank-money rails, the friction in moving between traditional and crypto-native finance drops further. The work to enable this is happening at the major settlement infrastructure providers.

**Yield products built on tokenised RWAs.** DeFi protocols are increasingly building structured yield products on tokenised treasuries, money market funds, and private credit. The risk-return profile is more conservative than traditional DeFi but more accessible than traditional fixed-income products.

**Regulatory expansion to new asset classes.** The current regulatory frameworks cover securities and money. They do not yet cleanly cover everything else. The next round of regulatory work will shape what can be tokenised compliantly in the second half of the decade.

## The honest assessment

Real-world asset tokenisation in 2026 is more real than the crypto cycle's history suggested it would ever be. It is also smaller and more specialised than the marketing claims. The categories where it works are real and growing; the categories where it does not work are also real and have not changed much.

The interesting question is not whether RWA tokenisation will continue to grow - it will - but which additional asset classes will follow the treasuries and private credit success stories versus which will follow the real estate and art disappointments. The pattern suggests the answer is "the ones where the traditional infrastructure is genuinely worse than the on-chain alternative," but applying that test to specific asset classes is the actual hard work.

The slow revolution is real. It just looks more like incremental institutional adoption of useful technology than like the revolutionary reorganisation of financial markets that the earlier rhetoric promised.
