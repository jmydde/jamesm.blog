The open web, as a specific historical artefact, had a beginning and might have an end. It started somewhere in the early 1990s, with the original public web that grew out of academic networking. It defined the shape of online life for thirty years: independent websites, hyperlinks between them, search engines that indexed them, browsers that rendered them, an underlying model where any user could navigate from any starting point to any other starting point through public hops.

In 2026, that model is under pressure from several directions at once. AI assistants increasingly mediate between users and information. Walled gardens dominate the mobile experience. Search has degraded as a discovery mechanism. The economics of running a quality independent website have gotten harder. Whether the open web is actually ending is a real question that does not get asked as often as it should.

## What "open" really meant

The web that was called "open" had several specific properties that are worth being explicit about:

- **Any URL was a valid destination.** A bookmark, a hyperlink, an email, a piece of writing - all could point at any web resource and that resource would be reachable.
- **Search worked.** Google and its competitors indexed the public web and made it discoverable. A user with a question could find resources that addressed it without going through any platform's recommendation system.
- **Browsing crossed sites freely.** A user clicking through links could end up on any of millions of websites, each with its own design, conventions, and content.
- **Publishing was unilateral.** Anyone with a domain and a web server could publish without seeking permission from any platform or gatekeeper.
- **Network effects were weaker than they would later become.** A small site could host valuable content and reach an audience without needing to be on any particular platform.

These properties combined to produce the specific cultural artefact that the open web was: a vast, decentralised, partly chaotic information layer that was meaningfully different from anything that came before or has come since.

## What is replacing it

The replacement is not a single thing. It is several things converging:

**AI-mediated information.** When a user asks a chatbot a question, they often get an answer that synthesises across many sources without sending the user to any of them. The information from the open web flows into the AI's training and inference; the user no longer visits the websites that produced it. The open web becomes infrastructure for AI rather than the destination users actually see.

**Walled gardens dominating mobile.** On a phone, most users spend most of their time in apps that have their own internal navigation and do not link out to the open web. The open web is a tab in a browser most users rarely open.

**Algorithmic feeds replacing search.** TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, X - the discovery model on these platforms is recommendation rather than search. Users find content by being shown things, not by querying for them.

**Quality content increasingly behind paywalls or in walled communities.** The economics of running an independent website depending on advertising revenue have deteriorated. Quality content increasingly moves to Substack subscriptions, Discord communities, Patreon-backed work - all behind some kind of gate, all less reachable from the public web.

**LLMs reducing the incentive to click through.** Why visit a website to read its content when you can ask the AI to summarise it? The behaviour shift, even modestly, hurts the websites that produced the content and over time makes them less viable.

The combined effect is that the share of human-information time spent on the genuinely open web has been declining for years and is continuing to decline.

## What might be lost

The open web is not unambiguously better than what is replacing it. Some of the changes are improvements - paywalled content tends to be higher quality than ad-supported content, walled communities can host better conversation than open forums - but several things the open web did do not have obvious successors:

**Stable, neutral references.** A Wikipedia article, a personal blog post, a small organisation's website - these were durable, unambiguous reference points. Citations could point at them. Conversations could refer to them. The replacement - AI-mediated answers that vary call to call - does not have this property.

**Discoverability of weird things.** The open web surfaced strange, niche, specialist content that no recommendation algorithm would promote. Whole subcultures grew up around obscure interests because the underlying infrastructure let weird content find its audience. The replacement infrastructure - recommendation algorithms tuned for engagement - does not surface weird content as well.

**The unilateral nature of publishing.** Anyone could put up a website without asking anyone's permission. The replacement systems all involve some kind of gatekeeper - the platform owner, the algorithm, the AI training set. The political and creative implications of this are non-trivial.

**The browser as a general-purpose tool.** The web browser, for all its accumulating complexity, is one of the few applications that does many things rather than one thing. Users have adopted it for tasks far beyond what the original designers intended. The replacement systems are more specialised and less flexible.

**The cultural artefact itself.** The open web at its best was a remarkable thing - a global, partly-anarchic, partly-collaborative information space that nobody owned. That specific cultural artefact is not necessarily reproducible in a different technical configuration. Once it goes, it might not come back.

## What might be preserved

A few things suggest that the open web's full disappearance is not inevitable:

**The infrastructure is still there.** Domains, web servers, HTTP, HTML, the underlying technical layer all continue to work. The decline is in usage, not in capability.

**There are deliberate counter-movements.** RSS readers are seeing a small revival. Personal blogs are being written again. The IndieWeb movement continues. The Fediverse provides an alternative to walled-garden social media that is web-native. None of these are mainstream, but they show that the alternative model is not extinct.

**Some functions of the open web are too important to fully privatise.** Government information, scientific publishing, civic services, reference materials - much of this still lives on the open web because the alternatives do not work for the function.

**The cost of running a small website is low and getting lower.** A static site on a CDN can be operated for almost nothing. The technical barriers to participation in the open web have not gone up - in many ways they have come down.

The open web might survive, but as a specialist layer rather than the mainstream interface. A bit like the way short-wave radio still exists but is no longer how most people get their news.

## What this asks of us

The interesting question for anyone who cares about this is what their own behaviour does to either side of the balance.

Every search query that goes to a chatbot instead of a search engine erodes the open web slightly. Every link click reinforces it. Every choice to publish on a personal website rather than a closed platform supports it. Every choice to subscribe to a newsletter instead of follow a feed has implications for which model wins.

The aggregate of these individual choices is what determines whether the open web continues to be a meaningful artefact or fades into a specialised backwater. It is not a top-down decision; it is the sum of millions of small ones.

The honest assessment in 2026 is that those small choices are mostly running against the open web. The trend lines are not encouraging. But trends are not destiny, and the open web has survived previous moments when its end seemed imminent. Whether it survives this one depends on whether enough people decide it is worth deliberately preserving rather than letting it dissipate by default.
