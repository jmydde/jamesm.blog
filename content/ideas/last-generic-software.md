Most software people use is generic. Spreadsheets that try to be useful for everything from family budgets to corporate forecasting. Project management tools that try to fit construction firms, software teams, and law firms. Email clients designed for billions of different communication patterns. The economics of building software has rewarded generality for the entire history of the industry - the marginal cost of distributing software is zero, the marginal cost of designing for the specific user is high, and the natural equilibrium has been to design for the average user and ship it to everyone.

The speculation in this essay is that this equilibrium is ending, and that the economic shift that has been quietly underway in 2025 and 2026 will produce a future where bespoke software - software designed for one specific person or team's exact workflow - becomes the default rather than the exception. If that happens, the consequences for the software industry are larger than the conversations about AI productivity tools have so far acknowledged.

## The economic argument

The reason generic software exists is that bespoke software has historically been expensive. A team of developers can build a workflow tool for a specific company at six-figure-and-up cost. The same team can build a SaaS product and sell it to ten thousand companies at lower per-company cost, even if each company gets a worse fit. The math has always favoured the SaaS approach for buyers and the SaaS approach for sellers.

AI changes both sides of this calculation. The cost of producing a bespoke tool for one team's workflow has dropped by, conservatively, an order of magnitude. The same drop applies to the cost of modifying that tool as the workflow evolves. The result is that the unit economics of bespoke software now compete with the unit economics of SaaS for many use cases, in a way they did not three years ago.

The exact threshold varies. For a tool used by a team of five with idiosyncratic workflow needs, bespoke is already cheaper. For a tool used by one person whose workflow is widely shared, SaaS is still cheaper. The threshold is moving, slowly, toward more bespoke and less generic. Where it ends up is the speculative part.

## What the post-generic world looks like

If the trend continues, the software landscape in five to ten years looks something like this:

**Generic SaaS survives for genuinely commodity problems.** Email, calendaring, video calls, basic document creation - these are workflows where most users want the same thing, where network effects matter, and where bespoke offers no real advantage. The big SaaS players in these categories will continue to exist.

**Generic SaaS loses ground in everything else.** Project management, CRM, knowledge management, accounting, inventory, internal tools - all the categories where users have historically complained about the fit of the off-the-shelf product to their actual workflow - shift toward bespoke. Not all at once, and not for everyone, but the trend line is clear.

**A new layer of software emerges**: lightweight bespoke tools, generated and maintained by AI, fitted to one team or one person. These tools are not "apps" in the traditional sense - they do not have brands, marketing pages, or millions of users. They exist for the specific user and disappear when no longer needed.

**The software industry restructures.** The economics of selling generic software to many customers is undermined when the marginal cost of bespoke approaches the price of the SaaS subscription. The companies that have built businesses on selling slightly-imperfect generic tools to millions of customers face a difficult transition.

This is speculative. The transition could stall - there are real friction points around data integration, security, and reliability that bespoke software historically has not handled well. But if it does not stall, the consequences are large.

## What is genuinely lost

A post-generic software world is not unambiguously better. A few things genuinely work better in the current arrangement:

- **Discoverability of practices.** A new analyst joining a company that runs on Salesforce learns the Salesforce way of working. The norms are portable. In a bespoke-software world, every team has its own conventions, and onboarding has to teach the conventions from scratch.
- **Cross-organisation interoperability.** Generic SaaS at least has APIs, schemas, and conventions that are shared across users. Bespoke software has whatever the AI generated, which may not align with any standard.
- **Network effects on data and integrations.** A SaaS tool used by many companies builds up an ecosystem of integrations, third-party reports, and benchmarks. A bespoke tool has none of these.
- **The shared user community.** Forums, training courses, conferences, certification programs - the social infrastructure around major SaaS products is real value. Bespoke software has no community.

These are not trivial. The case for generic software has always rested partly on these network effects, and they do not transfer to bespoke equivalents.

## The interesting consequence

The interesting consequence, if the speculation plays out, is that software starts to look more like writing - a thing that mostly exists in its specific form for a specific reader, with a small canon of widely-shared classics and a vast long tail of bespoke work. The current era, where a few dozen SaaS companies serve hundreds of millions of users with the same tools, would look in retrospect like a temporary economic accident produced by the high cost of bespoke production.

The temporary nature would not be obvious to people living through it - it has, after all, been the dominant pattern for the entire history of commercial software. But the underlying economic conditions that produced it are changing, and the equilibrium that resulted from those conditions is not guaranteed to survive.

This is not a prediction. It is a possibility worth taking seriously, because the cost of getting it wrong - building a generic-SaaS company in a world that is shifting toward bespoke - is high. The cost of preparing for it, even if it turns out to be slower or smaller than this essay suggests, is lower. The asymmetry favours engaging with the question.

The generic software era was a real era, with real benefits, and it has not ended yet. But the economic logic that produced it is no longer the dominant economic logic, and the era it produced will not last forever. Whatever replaces it will look quite different - and we are early enough in the transition that the shape is still up for grabs.
