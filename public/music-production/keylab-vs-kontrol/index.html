Arturia KeyLab 88 MK3 vs Native Instruments Kontrol S88 MK3¶

A detailed comparison of two flagship 88-key MIDI controllers aimed at serious players, producers, and composers.

1. Concept & Philosophy¶

Arturia KeyLab 88 MK3
A “do-everything” controller: hammer-action keybed, lots of hands-on controls (pads, faders, encoders), strong DAW integration, and wide plugin compatibility. It’s designed to be the centre of a studio rather than just a front-end for one company’s instruments.

Native Instruments Kontrol S88 MK3
A premium keyboard built primarily as the ultimate front-end for the NI ecosystem: Komplete, Kontakt libraries, and NKS-compatible instruments. It leans heavily into tight software integration, visual feedback, and smooth browsing rather than sheer quantity of hardware controls.

2. Keybed & Playability¶

KeyLab 88 MK3¶

  • 88-key hammer-action keybed with aftertouch.
  • Aimed at players who want a convincing piano-like feel for expressive playing.
  • Aftertouch lets you add vibrato/modulation without grabbing a wheel or knob.
  • Suits: pianists, film composers, and anyone who wants nuanced dynamic control.

Kontrol S88 MK3¶

  • 88-key fully weighted keybed (Fatar-style, premium feel).
  • Also designed to feel “piano-correct,” but with an emphasis on consistency and reliability in a studio setting.
  • No drum pads or faders to clutter the playing surface – the keybed is the star.
  • Suits: players who live inside Kontakt / Komplete and want a high-end “master” keyboard.

Summary: Both feel like proper pianos rather than synths. The deciding factor is less about raw feel and more about what you want around the keys: lots of controls (Arturia) vs a cleaner, more focused interface (NI).

3. Controls, Layout, and Workflow¶

KeyLab 88 MK3¶

  • Pads: RGB drum pads for finger-drumming, clip launching, or triggering scenes.
  • Faders & Knobs: Multiple faders and rotary encoders mapped to DAW mixer controls, synth parameters, etc.
  • Transport & DAW control: Play, stop, record, loop, etc. Many DAWs have templates or scripts for tight integration.
  • Performance tools:
    • Chord modes
    • Scale modes
    • Arpeggiator
    • Split and layer setups
  • Extra hardware touches: Music stand, laptop stand, optional frame for a self-contained “station.”

Kontrol S88 MK3¶

  • Fewer physical controls: Minimal set of encoders and buttons. No drum pads, fewer faders.
  • High-resolution displays: Core part of the workflow. You browse sounds, see parameter names, and navigate libraries from the keyboard itself.
  • Light Guide: Per-key LEDs that can show key switches, scales, drum mappings, etc. Very useful for complex Kontakt instruments.
  • NKS integration: Parameters of NKS-compatible instruments are pre-mapped and shown on the screens with sensible pages (Filter, Env, FX, etc.).
  • Transport control: Available for major DAWs, but less of a “full DAW mixer” surface than the KeyLab.

Summary:

  • KeyLab = lots of hardware controls and a traditional MIDI controller vibe.
  • S88 = a refined, screen-driven interface where the magic is in browsing and controlling virtual instruments smoothly.

4. Software Integration & Ecosystem¶

KeyLab 88 MK3¶

  • Works with almost anything via standard MIDI and DAW integration scripts.
  • Strong mapping to Arturia’s own Analog Lab and V Collection instruments.
  • Good “generic” DAW templates, often including: Ableton Live, Logic, Cubase, Studio One, Bitwig, etc.
  • You’re not locked into a single ecosystem; it’s designed to play nicely with a wide range of plugins.

Kontrol S88 MK3¶

  • Deep, polished integration with:
    • Komplete
    • Kontakt libraries
    • NKS instruments from NI and third parties
  • Browser lets you filter by category, character, vendor, etc., and load instruments without touching the mouse.
  • Parameters are automatically mapped and named, so you spend less time MIDI-learning and more time playing.
  • Outside the NI/NKS world it still works as a standard MIDI keyboard, but you lose a lot of what makes it special.

Summary:

  • If your world = Kontakt + Komplete + NKS, the S88 feels like home.
  • If you hop between many different VSTs and don’t want one company at the centre of your world, the KeyLab is more neutral and flexible.

5. Build Quality & Design¶

KeyLab 88 MK3¶

  • Metal chassis with wood side panels: feels solid and “instrument-like.”
  • Slightly busier front panel because of all the pads, faders, and knobs.
  • Strengths: durability, lots of physical control, studio-hub aesthetic.

Kontrol S88 MK3¶

  • Clean, modern look with more emphasis on screens and light guides.
  • Fewer visible controls, which some people love (minimalist) and some find limiting.
  • Feels premium and slick, especially when used with NI software on a big studio setup.

6. Use Cases¶

Choose KeyLab 88 MK3 if you:

  • Want one keyboard to rule everything: piano playing, DAW control, pads, faders, synth tweaking.
  • Move between multiple DAWs and plugins and don’t want to be locked into one ecosystem.
  • Do a bit of everything: writing, producing, mixing, maybe even live performance.

Choose Kontrol S88 MK3 if you:

  • Are heavily invested in Komplete / Kontakt / NKS libraries.
  • Spend lots of time browsing presets and swapping instruments, and want this process to be fast and visual from the keyboard.
  • Prefer a clean, uncluttered front panel and rely more on screens and pre-mapped controls than on a ton of physical faders and pads.

7. Pros & Cons Summary¶

Arturia KeyLab 88 MK3 – Pros¶

  • Realistic hammer-action keybed with aftertouch.
  • Lots of physical controls (pads, faders, knobs).
  • Strong DAW and plugin flexibility.
  • Great as a central studio controller.

Arturia KeyLab 88 MK3 – Cons¶

  • Busy front panel; overkill if you don’t use many controls.
  • DAW integration quality can vary by platform.
  • Less “deep” with any single ecosystem than NI is with its own.

Native Instruments Kontrol S88 MK3 – Pros¶

  • Outstanding integration with NI/Komplete/Kontakt/NKS.
  • Excellent screens and light guide for feedback and navigation.
  • Premium keybed and polished workflow for virtual instruments.
  • Superb for cinematic, orchestral, and sound-design work.

Native Instruments Kontrol S88 MK3 – Cons¶

  • Fewer hardware controls; no pads and fewer faders.
  • Best features heavily depend on using NI software.
  • Less suited as a “do-everything” DAW controller surface.

8. Verdict¶

Both are excellent 88-key controllers, but they are optimised for different worlds:

  • KeyLab 88 MK3 is the better choice if you want a versatile, DAW-centric studio hub with lots of hands-on control and a very playable hammer-action keybed.
  • Kontrol S88 MK3 shines if you live in the Native Instruments universe, want deep integration with sample libraries and NKS instruments, and value screen-driven browsing and parameter control over a large number of hardware controls.

In short:

  • Generalist, hardware-rich all-rounder? → Arturia KeyLab 88 MK3
  • NI-centric, instrument-browsing powerhouse? → Native Instruments Kontrol S88 MK3
